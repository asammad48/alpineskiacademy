To Rent or to Buy? The Smartest Choice

For many, the question of whether to rent or buy is one of the most important. The answer is simple: for most skiers, renting is the smartest option. Not only do you save a significant initial investment and the cost of annual maintenance, but you also have the freedom to try out the latest generation equipment each season. Our partners constantly renew their equipment, which always guarantees you the best, without having to worry about the logistics of transport or storage.

The Importance of Quality Equipment

Renting your equipment may seem simple, but the right choice makes a big difference in your performance and, above all, in your safety on the slopes. Quality, modern, and well-maintained equipment offers multiple benefits. Therefore, we recommend renting at prestigious establishments that are also official distributors of leading brands, which ensures that their equipment is state-of-the-art and constantly renewed.

  • Greater safety: Modern skis or a board, in good condition, with sharp edges, smooth and well-waxed bases, and well-adjusted bindings, give you the control you need to avoid accidents.
  • Better progression: If you are a beginner, equipment adapted to your level helps you learn faster and with less effort. If you are an advanced skier, high-end equipment allows you to perfect your technique and continue to progress, giving you better sensations in every turn or terrain you slide on.
  • Full enjoyment: Well-fitting boots and appropriate equipment eliminate discomfort, allowing you to ski longer and enjoy every descent without worries.

Choice Guide: The Key is in the Detail

Choosing your equipment is not a matter of luck, but of knowledge. Here we give you some expert tips so you know what to ask for in the store. Of course, if we have already skied together, do not hesitate to tell us before renting or buying so we can advise you in a completely personalized way.

1. Skis: The Definitive Guide by Level

Skis for Beginners: First Steps with Confidence

  • Length (Size): The golden rule is to choose skis 10 to 15 cm shorter than your height. This reduces the weight at the tip and tail, facilitating directional control and the transition from one turn to another.
  • Turning Radius: Beginner skis usually have a shorter turning radius (between 12 and 15 meters). A short turning radius facilitates entry into the turn without needing to exert much pressure or speed.
  • Weight and Reaction: They are lighter and made of softer materials. At low speeds, this flexibility absorbs irregularities and forgives technical errors, preventing the ski from reacting abruptly.
  • Waist (Width): The waist (the central part of the ski under the boot) is usually of medium width (around 70-75 mm). This provides a stable support surface on groomed snow.

Skis for Intermediate Level: Gaining Ground and Speed

  • Length (Size): The recommended length is closer to the skier's height, typically between 5 and 10 cm less. This provides greater stability in wide turns and at higher speeds.
  • Turning Radius: It is in a medium range (between 14 and 17 meters). A slightly longer radius allows for greater control in high-speed turns without sacrificing agility.
  • Weight and Reaction: They incorporate higher quality materials and a more robust construction. They may include a layer of carbon fiber or titanal to increase rigidity. This allows the ski to react with more energy at the exit of the turn.
  • Waist (Width): The waist remains at a similar or slightly wider width (75-85 mm), as at this level the skier ventures into a greater variety of terrains.

Skis for High and Expert Level: Power, Precision, and Specialization

  • Length and Skiing Style: The size is no longer defined only by height, but by the chosen discipline: Slalom (SL) are shorter (between 155 and 165 cm), Giant (GS) are longer (between 175 and 195 cm), and Freeride/All-Mountain is a personal choice.
  • Waist Width: This factor defines the specialty: Narrow Waist (66-70 mm) for piste, Medium Waist (70-86 mm) for All-Mountain, and Wide Waist (86-120 mm and more) for Freeride and powder snow.
  • Materials and Reaction: High-end skis incorporate double layers of Titanal, carbon, and other fibers that increase their rigidity. They require a refined technique to make them work, offering in return explosive power and reaction.

2. The Boots: Your Connection to the Ski

The boot is the fundamental link that transmits every movement of your body to the snow. A perfect fit not only eliminates pain, but also prevents injuries and ensures maximum precision.

  • The Flex of the Boot: The measure of rigidity. It ranges from 30 to 60 for children and beginners, from 60 to 100 for intermediate levels, and from 100 to 140+ for expert skiers. A higher flex translates into a faster and more powerful return at the exit of the turn.
  • The Last (Width): The width of the boot. A narrow last (97-98 mm) offers maximum precision, while a standard last (99-101 mm) balances comfort and control, and a wide last (102-103 mm) prioritizes comfort.
  • Rental vs. Sale: Rental boots use softer plastics for durability, sacrificing some precision. Sale boots, on the other hand, use harder plastics and thermoformable or heated liners for instant and precise energy transmission.

Rental tip: always try several sizes and never choose a boot that is too large "for comfort": it compromises control and increases the risk of injury.

3. The Poles: Your Balancing Tool

The height of the pole is vital for an efficient technique. An incorrect measurement can subtly alter your position on the skis.

  • Standard Measurement: Invert the pole, hold it below the basket, and rest it on the ground. Your arm should form a 90-degree angle.
  • Short Poles: They facilitate tight turns and are ideal for bumpy areas, allowing for fluid movement.
  • Long Poles: They provide greater momentum on flat terrain and are useful for maintaining rhythm in wide turns.

4. Snowboard Equipment: Modality, Flex, and Board Profile

When choosing a rental snowboard, it is advisable to pay attention to three key aspects: the modality, the flex (rigidity), and the profile (camber or rocker).

Board Flex

The flex is measured on a scale of 1 to 10:

  • Soft (1-4): Easy to handle and forgiving of mistakes. Recommended for beginners and for freestyle in the snowpark.
  • Medium (4-7): Versatile and stable on all types of terrain. Ideal for "all-mountain" use.
  • Stiff (7-10): Very stable at high speed and with great grip on hard snow. Recommended for freeride and advanced riders.

Board Profile (camber/rocker)

The profile determines the behavior of the board on the snow:

  • Camber (positive curvature): Greater grip and response, perfect for piste and carving.
  • Rocker (negative curvature): Facilitates turns and floats better in powder snow. Ideal for beginners and freeride.
  • Hybrid (camber + rocker): Combines grip and flotation. It is the most versatile option in rental.

Modality and recommended use

  • Piste: More precise and faster boards, with good edge grip.
  • Freestyle: Twin-tip boards, maneuverable and with a softer flex for jumps and tricks.
  • Freeride: Directional boards, with a longer nose and rocker at the tip to improve flotation in deep snow.

Practical tips for choosing a board in the rental

  • Length: As a general rule, the board should reach between the chin and the nose when placed upright next to the body. Shorter → easier to turn, recommended for beginners and freestyle. Longer → more stable at speed and with greater flotation in powder snow, ideal for freeride.
  • Waist width: It must be adjusted to the boot size. Boots < 42 EU → standard board. Boots 43 EU or more → wide board (wider) to prevent the feet from sticking out and catching on the snow.
  • Experience level: Beginner → soft flex and rocker or simple hybrid. Intermediate → medium flex and hybrid. Advanced → medium-stiff flex and camber/hybrid depending on the modality.

Quick summary for renting

  • If you are a beginner → short board, soft flex, rocker or hybrid profile.
  • If you are looking for a versatile board → medium length, medium flex, hybrid profile.
  • If you want freeride or carving at speed → longer board, stiff flex, camber or hybrid profile with nose rocker.
