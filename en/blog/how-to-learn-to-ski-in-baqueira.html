The Excitement of Your First Day on the Snow

We've all been there: the mix of excitement and nerves when you put on ski boots for the first time. It's normal to wonder: will I be able to do it?, what if I fall?, will I bother others on the slope? The good news is that learning to ski is easier than it seems if you start with the right technique and well-adjusted equipment.

This guide is designed for you, who want to enjoy Baqueira from day one, learning to control your speed, stop, and make your first turns with confidence.

The Equipment and the Basic Stance

Learning to ski for beginners starts long before you get on the slope. Proper adaptation to the equipment is key for you to feel safe and progress from the first day.

Choosing the right equipment for beginners

  • Skis: For beginners, they should ideally be 10 to 15 cm shorter than your height. This makes control and turns easier.
  • Boots: They should hold your foot firmly without pain or pressure points. A boot that is too loose reduces control and safety.
  • Poles: Adjust the height so that when you stick them in the snow, your arm forms a 90Â° angle.
  • Helmet and goggles: Essential for safety and visibility on the mountain. Don't forget sunscreen!

ðŸ‘‰ Practical tip: Always ask for help at the rental shop or from your instructor; correct adjustment of the bindings is key to your safety and so that the skis release in case of a fall. If you need help with the equipment, check out our tips for ski equipment rental.

The basic skier's stance

  • Knees and hips semi-flexed and ankles flexed (feel the shins resting on the boot tongues, trying to feel that the weight of the trunk and hips are supported in the center of the feet through these joint flexions).
  • Weight centered on the sole of the foot, never on the heels or excessively forward, the opening of the legs should be natural to the width of the hips, neither too close nor too far apart.
  • Arms slightly in front of the body and to the sides (outside the skis), with poles pointing backwards, preventing the tips from sticking into the snow.

After learning to put on the skis correctly, there are many exercises that we can practice with one and two skis on the flat in which we will strengthen that basic posture and begin to have the first sensations with the equipment on without any risk. That is why it is good to start with a qualified teacher who will guide you at this point of learning.

The Glide and the First Stop

Once you feel comfortable in the basic stance, it's time to glide on a gentle slope, always with a counter-slope at the end to avoid any risk or unwanted fall.

  • Direct descent: Let gravity take you, maintaining your balance posture.
  • The wedge or "pizza": Bring the tips of the skis together and open the tails in a V-shape. This is your first tool to stop. You have to try to feel how the cutting parts of the ski or edges gently rub against the snow from the inside of the sole of your feet and open slightly behind. That gesture is what will give you security when you master it. Be very gentle and progressive in the execution of your movements.
  • Stopping in a wedge: The more you open the V, the more friction you will have and the faster you will reduce speed. Try to distribute the weight equally on both feet, keeping the load centered, try not to lean your body too far back or you will lose control. Also try to ensure that the tips of the skis (spatulas) do not overlap each other, both skis must always be in contact with the snow.

The first great achievement is discovering that you are the one who decides when to stop. That feeling of control is the key to starting to enjoy skiing.

The First Turns

Mastering turns is the most exciting moment of your beginnings.

  • Wedge turns: To turn right, put more weight on the left ski and slightly open the tail of that ski very gently and exerting a little angle with the inner part of the foot; to turn left, do the same on the right ski.
  • Body orientation: Accompany the turn by looking where you want to go. Like when you drive: your body guides the direction.
  • Natural progression: Little by little your turns will become more fluid, until the wedge transforms into parallel and you begin to slide with ease.

ðŸ‘‰ Tip: Where you look, you go. (It almost always works)

For each moment of evolution there are specific educational exercises and concrete tips for each new gesture or learning. A good teacher to guide you will greatly reduce your learning hours in addition to being correct and with a solid foundation. You have to know what to do and how to do it and having an expert to help you makes the difference.

Frequently Asked Questions for Beginners

Which slope is the best to start on?

You should always start on the beginner slopes and then the green slopes. They are the gentlest, widest, and safest for your first descents. In our beginner lessons you will find more details.

Will I fall a lot?

It depends! Whether you are a child or an adult, if you learn on your own, you will fall, but it is normal and part of the learning process. Falls on beginner slopes are usually minor and will help you gain confidence. If you hire a good instructor, it will greatly reduce the number of falls, especially in a private lesson. In a group it will also do so, but less, since the attention is divided among the rest of the participants. Even so, the advice, the guide, the feedback and the proposed exercises are always of great help.

Is it better to learn alone or with a teacher?

Always with a professional instructor. You will avoid bad habits, progress faster, and ski with greater safety from the beginning. The answer is a resounding yes. At Alpine Ski Academy, our instructors not only teach you the technique, but they guarantee safe and fast learning, adapted to your pace. In addition, the instructor will always choose the best and most appropriate terrain for the moment you are in and your real needs both in will and in execution capacity so that the learning is as effective and appropriate as possible. This ability to assess is non-existent in a beginner. Meet our team of instructors.

How long does it take to learn to ski?

With about 10 hours of lessons (approx. one week), you will be able to go down green and blue slopes with safety and control. As always, each person is different and there are very skilled people who are able to ski on blue slopes in 4 hours and others who will require 15-20h for the same goal. You can see our prices for ski or snowboard lessons on our website.

What clothes and equipment do I need?

The ideal is to dress in layers (thermal, fleece and waterproof jacket and pants). Do not forget a helmet, waterproof gloves, a buff, and sunglasses or goggles. Always take into account the weather forecast, the sun, the wind, the humidity and the temperature are factors that can make you choose a certain amount of clothing or another.
